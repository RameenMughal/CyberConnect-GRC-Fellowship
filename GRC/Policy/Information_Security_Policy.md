## Overview

Policy: Plan or course of action as of government, political party or business, intended to influence and determine decisions, actions and other matters.

Policy is the intent of the management.

Policy is the expectation of the management.

Policies are written to support the mission, vision and strategic planning of an organization.

Difference between Law and Policy:
- Law is framed for bringing justice to the society, a policy is framed for achieving certain goals.
- Laws are for the people, and policies are made in the name of the people.
- Policies can be called a set of rules that guide any government or any organization.

Privacy regulation named GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation).

For Example: When a company wants to be compliant to GDPR, having 50 employees so it will be unfeasible to tell each employee the GDPR rules and controls, so we use Policy having all the information and instructions that will be given to these employees.

When all the employees follow the instructions, we automatically be compliant to the GDRP.

Security Policies are established rules that provide guidance in the protection of an organization's assets.

Information Security Policy provides rules for the protection of the information assets of the organization.

People are on the Operation Level.

Senior Level Positions are on the Mission Level, on how to make the organization good so we make policy to apply this on people that work on Operation Level.

## Policy Hierarchy

- **Principle**: Highest-Level ideas and values
  - Attitudes, values that characterizes the company
  - Guiding principles set the tone for the corporate culture
  - For Example: When you want the company to respect every culture and community, you first create a vision or mission.
- **Policy**: High - level information, mandatory statements
  - To apply the vision, you create policy
  - Policy uses the word "must" which makes it mandatoy to follow
- **Standard**: Prescried levels or criteria
  - For Example: You want to make a Password Policy but you need to specify like what password, what type of password do you want, so you make a Sandard
- **Guidelines**: Best practices designed to achieve policy objectives
  - It is not mandatory to follow but considered good
  - For Example: Specify what can be the strong password, how you can do this
- **Baseline**: Minimum configuration requirements
  - For Example: Specify on how the admin can ensure that every employee will have unique User ID and strong password like in Active Directory.
  - Not necessary in the Policy
  - Technical implementation. It is specific to a particular piece of technology (like Windows, a Cisco router, or a Linux server).
  - It tells the system administrator exactly which settings to toggle to meet the Standard.
- **Procedures**: Individual, simple and discrete steps which the people will follow to meet the standard

## Create Policy

- Identify the applicable legal and regulatory requirements
- **GLBA** Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act: Requires financial institutions and other covered entities to develop and adhere to an information security policy that protects customer information and assigns responsibility for the adherence to the Board of Directors.
- **Health Insurance Portability and Accoutability Act**: Covered entities are required to publish comprehensive information security policies that communicate in detail how information is protected.
- **PCI DSS** (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard): A strong security policy sets the tone for security affecting an organization's entire company, and it informs employees of their expected duties related to security
